Which deity is to be worshipped during Kumbh?

The Kumbh Mela, a spiritual gathering that occurs once every 12 years, is deeply rooted in the religious and cultural traditions of Hinduism. During this grand event, millions of pilgrims, sages, and ascetics converge to take part in a sacred bath at the confluence of holy rivers, which is believed to purify the mind, body, and soul. While the Mela is a time for collective devotion and reflection, it is also a time to honor specific deities, whose worship forms the core of the event’s spiritual essence.

1. Worship of Lord Shiva: The Supreme Deity at Kumbh Mela

One of the primary deities worshipped during the Kumbh Mela is Lord Shiva, revered as the supreme destroyer and transformer within the Hindu Trinity (Trimurti). As the one who embodies both creation and destruction, Lord Shiva's presence during Kumbh is a reminder of the cycle of life, death, and rebirth.

At the various Kumbh Mela sites, Lord Shiva's worship is prevalent. Notably, Kumbheshwar at Kumbhakonam, in Tamil Nadu, stands as an example of the reverence shown towards Lord Shiva. The temple here houses a Shiva Lingam in the form of a pitcher (Kumbha), which is a symbolic representation of the divine essence contained within. This association connects the Kumbh with the concept of the pitcher of nectar or amrita, linking it to the ancient myth of the churning of the ocean.

In many of the temples where the Kumbh Mela takes place, Lord Shiva is often worshipped through rituals that include offerings, abhishekam (ritual bathing of the deity with water, milk, and other sacred liquids), and recitations of sacred texts. His divine presence is central to the spiritual atmosphere of Kumbh Mela, where pilgrims come to seek his blessings for peace, liberation, and spiritual growth.

Shloka for Lord Shiva:

"ॐ नमः शिवाय।"
"Om Namah Shivaya"
Translation: "I bow to Lord Shiva."

This simple yet profound mantra is widely chanted by devotees, signifying surrender to the divine energy of Lord Shiva, who represents both destruction and transformation.

2. Worship of Lord Vishnu: The Preserver of the Universe

Another key deity venerated during the Kumbh Mela is Lord Vishnu, who is seen as the preserver of the universe in Hindu mythology. As the deity who maintains the cosmic order, Vishnu's blessings are sought to sustain both the physical and spiritual realms. His worship is integral to many of the rituals at Kumbh Mela, especially for those who follow the Vaishnavite tradition.

At the Kumbh Mela, devotees chant praises of Lord Vishnu, seeking protection, guidance, and balance in life. The Vishnu Sahasranama, a litany of a thousand names of Lord Vishnu, is often recited in devotion.

Shloka for Lord Vishnu:

"ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय।"
"Om Namo Bhagavate Vasudevaya"
Translation: "I bow to Lord Vasudeva (Vishnu), the divine incarnation."

This powerful mantra is a common invocation during the Kumbh, signifying the devotee’s deep surrender to Lord Vishnu and his role in the preservation of the cosmos.

3. Worship of Goddess Parvati: The Divine Mother

In the context of the Kumbh Mela at Kumbhakonam, a notable feature is the worship of Goddess Parvati, who is honored as Mangalambika, the consort of Lord Shiva. Parvati represents the divine feminine energy and is considered a symbol of fertility, compassion, and love.

Her worship during the Kumbh is significant, as it completes the worship of the divine trinity: Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva. At the Kumbh Mela, many devotees turn to Goddess Parvati for strength, guidance, and blessings for a prosperous life.

Shloka from Devi Mahatmyam (Durga Saptashati)

"या देवी सर्वभूतेषु मातरूपेण संस्थिता।
नमस्तस्यै नमस्तस्यै नमस्तस्यै नमो नमः॥"

(Devi Mahatmyam 1.1)

Transliteration:
"Yā devī sarvabhūteṣu mātarūpeṇa sansthitā,
Namastasyai namastasyai namastasyai namo namaḥ."

Translation:
"Salutations to the Goddess, who resides in all beings in the form of the Divine Mother. We bow to Her, we bow to Her, we bow to Her again and again."

This shloka beautifully expresses the reverence for Devi in her form as the Divine Mother. Goddess Parvati, as Mangalambika, is worshipped as the mother of the universe, embodying compassion, nurturing energy, and the essence of divine femininity. The repetition of "Namastasyai" highlights the humility and devotion of the worshippers as they seek Her blessings for guidance, strength, and prosperity, just as many devotees do during the Kumbh Mela.

This shloka captures the essence of Parvati's blessings, offering a prayer to Her as the Divine Mother, who ensures the well-being and protection of Her children, much like how devotees approach Her during the auspicious occasion of the Kumbh.

4. Worship of the Sacred Confluence of Rivers: The Personification of Divinity

While specific deities like Shiva and Vishnu are worshipped, the confluence of the rivers at each Kumbh Mela site is also treated as a sacred and living deity. At Prayag (Allahabad), the Sangam (the meeting point of the Ganga, Yamuna, and the mystical Saraswati) is regarded as an embodiment of divinity. The act of bathing in these holy waters is believed to cleanse the devotee’s soul and bring them closer to liberation (moksha).

The rivers are symbolically connected to various divine figures in Hinduism. For example, Ganga is personified as the river goddess who descends from the heavens to Earth, carrying the holy waters that purify the mind and body. Yamuna is revered as the playful and beloved sister of Yama, the god of death, offering the gift of eternal life through the sacred bath.

5. Worship of the Trimurti (Brahma, Vishnu, Shiva)

The concept of the Trimurti, which consists of Brahma (the creator), Vishnu (the preserver), and Shiva (the destroyer), forms the foundation of the spiritual ethos of the Kumbh Mela. The confluence of these three deities is symbolized by the worship of the Sangam at Prayag. Each of these deities represents a key aspect of the universe's functioning: creation, preservation, and destruction.

Brahma is particularly significant in the origin story of the Kumbh Mela, where it is believed that Brahma, in the distant past, initiated the sacred bath to cleanse the universe during a period of cosmic imbalance. As such, his presence is acknowledged during Kumbh through prayers and rituals that honor the cyclical nature of life.
